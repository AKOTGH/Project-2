Sharath Ragila's Tech Hero

Kimberly Bryant is my tech hero. She is a technology leader and social entrepreneur best known as the founder of Black Girls Code, a nonprofit organization dedicated to increasing opportunities for Black girls and women in computer science and related STEM fields.

Bryant founded Black Girls Code after noticing a severe lack of racial and gender diversity in technical spaces, including those her own daughter was entering. Rather than accepting these barriers as inevitable, she focused on early intervention—providing young students with coding education, mentorship, and a supportive community long before college or the workforce.

Her work directly confronts implicit bias in education and technology. Many technology pipelines unintentionally favor students who already have access to resources, encouragement, and representation. Black Girls Code challenges these assumptions by demonstrating that talent is universal, while opportunity is not.

Bryant’s leadership is also deeply intersectional. Her programs recognize that race and gender intersect in ways that create unique barriers for Black girls, particularly in environments where they are often underrepresented or stereotyped. By centering these experiences, her work goes beyond surface-level diversity efforts and instead builds long-term structural change.

Kimberly Bryant exemplifies how technology leadership can be used to advance equity, disrupt bias, and create inclusive futures—making her an ideal example of a tech hero who challenges norms rather than reinforcing them.

Alexander Kaminsky’s Tech Hero

Yeshimabeit Milner is my tech hero. She is a data technologist, activist, and the founder and executive director of Data for Black Lives (D4BL). Her organization is a movement of activists, organizers, and mathematicians committed to using data science to create concrete and measurable change in the lives of marginalized communities.

Milner challenges the standard tech industry norm that treats data merely as a commodity to be extracted. She recognizes that historical biases are often hardcoded into the algorithms used for credit scoring, predictive policing, and algorithmic pricing. These systems frequently operate as "black boxes" that bypass consumer protection and disproportionately harm people of color. Rather than rejecting data science, Milner’s work focuses on reclaiming it, advocating for data to be used as a tool for social change and equity rather than a weapon of systemic oppression.

Under her leadership, D4BL has pushed for critical accountability in tech, demanding transparency in how algorithms make high-stakes decisions about people's lives. She emphasizes that algorithms are not neutral math; they are built by people with inherent biases. By bridging the gap between tech experts and community organizers, she is fighting to ensure that consumer welfare and civil rights are at the forefront of technological development.

I chose Milner because she exemplifies what it means to be a leader in advancing equity in technology today. She is not a Silicon Valley CEO building apps for convenience; she is a trailblazer demanding that our data infrastructure respects and protects the public.

Vesa Ljumani’s Tech Hero

Bridgit Mendler is my tech hero because she completely redefined what a career in technology can look like. She seamlessly transitioned from entertainment into building and leading in the tech world, cofounding a satellite data startup while also pursuing advanced degrees at MIT and Harvard. Her path proves that creativity, curiosity, and ambition belong in tech just as much as coding, and she inspires me to believe that you do not have to fit one mold to make a real impact in the industry.
