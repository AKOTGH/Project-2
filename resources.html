What Is Implicit Bias?

Implicit bias refers to unconscious attitudes or stereotypes that influence how people perceive, evaluate, and interact with others. These biases operate automatically and often contradict our consciously held values or beliefs.

Implicit biases develop through repeated exposure to cultural messages, media representations, social norms, and institutional practices. Over time, the brain learns shortcuts to process information quickly—sometimes at the cost of fairness or accuracy.

In academic, professional, and technological contexts, implicit bias can affect grading, hiring decisions, user experience design, algorithmic outcomes, and access to resources. Because these biases are unconscious, they can be especially difficult to identify without intentional reflection.

What Is Intersectionality?

Intersectionality is a framework for understanding how multiple aspects of identity—such as race, gender, class, sexuality, and ability—interact to shape lived experiences and access to power.

Rather than viewing identity categories in isolation, intersectionality emphasizes that systems of inequality overlap. For example, the experiences of a Black woman in technology cannot be fully understood by examining race or gender alone.

Intersectionality is essential for understanding implicit bias because biases often operate differently depending on context. A one-size-fits-all approach to diversity can unintentionally ignore the people most affected by overlapping forms of discrimination.

Why These Concepts Matter in Technology

Technology is often viewed as neutral or objective, but it is shaped by human decisions. Implicit bias can influence which problems are prioritized, whose voices are included, and how systems are designed.

When designers and developers fail to consider intersectionality, technologies can unintentionally reinforce inequality—such as biased algorithms, inaccessible interfaces, or exclusionary data sets. Awareness is a critical first step toward more ethical and inclusive technological systems.
